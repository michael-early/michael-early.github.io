Fast-Food Minimum Wage (AB 1228)

Policy evaluation · Difference-in-differences · Labor economics

Problem

California’s AB 1228 raised the fast-food minimum wage to $20. Policymakers and employers needed evidence on short-run employment effects, especially for youth. I asked: what was the immediate impact on youth employment in California relative to comparable states?

Method

I used CPS ORG microdata (IPUMS) for California and bordering control states, focusing on ages 16–24. I ran a difference-in-differences design with robust standard errors to estimate the causal effect of the policy on youth employment.

Result

No immediate collapse in youth employment. Effects were heterogeneous by metro area, and results were consistent with partial adjustment rather than large disemployment. Full specification, robustness checks, and discussion are in the working paper below.
